Trainer's Responsibilities and
Expectations

Your role in properly setting the tone for the class and positioning the importance of safe use is critical. As the trainer, it is your responsibility to provide the necessary information so that each operator fully understands their responsibilities associated with the safe operation of telehandlers.

For operators to pass this course, they must show proficiency in a hands-on demonstration of safe operation and achieve a score of 100% on the written exam.

In order to improve your chances of success, you should have an understanding of your operator's application and working environment.

Whenever possible, promote discussions of situations based on actual experiences. Making this course relevant to the working world will greatly improve the likelihood that the operators will take what they have learned in your classroom back to their respective jobs.

Remember, your ultimate goal is to ensure that operators fully understand the procedures and practices of safe telehandler operation, as well as all workplace factors which may affect their safety and the safety of those around them.

qualified person

Before a trainer can conduct a training course in the safe operation of a telehandler, they must be deemed qualified by their employer.

The American National Standards Institute (ANSI) defines a qualified person as:

"One who, by possession of a recognized degree, certificate, or professional standing, or by extensive knowledge, training, and experience, has successfully demonstrated his/her ability to solve or resolve problems related to the subject matter, the work, or the project.“

Not only must a trainer meet the definition of a qualified trainer, they must also comply with the responsibilities regarding training as outlined in ANSI/ITSDF B56.6, OSHA Rule 1910.178 (I) and CSA B335 standards, including record keeping and determination that operators have successfully demonstrated that they are qualified to operate a telehandler.

Class 1–5 Forklifts: Forklift operator training requirements are addressed in OSHA 1910.178(l). For forklifts (Classes 1–5), the ANSI/ITSDF standard is typically B56.1.

Operator's Responsibilities and
Expectations

It is the operator's responsibility to fully understand all responsibilities associated with the safe operation of telehandlers.

As mentioned previously, each operator must achieve 100% on the written examination and show proficiency in the hands-on demonstration of safe machine operation. Anything less than 100% would imply that it's okay to compromise jobsite safety, which is never acceptable. Each operator must actively participate in the training workshop, including classroom activities and hands-on operation.

Only trained personnel should be allowed to operate a telehandler. If an operator does not meet the established standard for either the hands-on demonstration or the written exam, the trainer must inform the trainee's employer.

Additional training and/or coaching may be necessary to pass the course.

By successfully completing this course, the operator will also be expected to identify and correct any unsafe operation or potential accident observed of other operators.

Safety is Serious Business

Safety is not a subject that should be taken lightly. It is important to establish with the class the severity of consequences if safety is not a priority on any worksite.

It cannot be overstated that the safe operation of a telehandler on any worksite can save the life of the operator as well as those working around them. Therefore, the responsibilities are great for both the trainer and operator to ensure that the safe operation of a telehandler is always of paramount importance.

Course Preparation

In this section we will cover some helpful hints and tools that you can use before presenting any training that will make your class time more consistent and effective.

When applying these suggestions, it is important to be flexible and to adjust your training to fit the special needs of your class as the situation demands. To be an effective trainer, you will need to be fully prepared prior to conducting the training class.

You should include the following as a minimum in your course preparation:

  • Have a quiet, well-lit inside training area that can accommodate the class size and training aids, and an outside training area capable of accommodating the operators, at least one telehandler, and the practical test course.
  • Know the course content, the telehandler(s) that will be used, applicable operator's manual(s), machine specifications, and the standards governing Class 7 Rough Terrain Forklifts.
  • Ensure that all telehandlers that will be used for the practical demonstration portion are properly maintained and operating as designed.
  • Ensure that classroom supplies and materials are available and adequate.
  • Prepare the outdoor hands-on training area by placing pallets and other training aids in place ahead of time.

Areas of Focus

Some specific areas we will focus on in course preparation are:

  • training agenda
  • training format
  • Using the training guide
  • training location considerations
  • training materials

training agenda

Developing a training agenda (or organized plan) that will cover the content that you will provide during your training will go a long way to improving the success of your telehandler safety training class. Let's review some helpful hints on how to develop your agenda:

  • Determine in advance how long the training session will last. Duration may vary depending on class size and number of models to be covered.
  • Know your operator's experience, and any potential language barriers.
  • The demonstration portion of the training may vary in time more than the classroom training. Be prepared for this and be sure to allow plenty of time so that it is not rushed.
  • Try to schedule the class in the morning when the operators can give you their peak attention.
  • If the class size is too large, try "team teaching“ with another trainer. Split the class into two or more groups. This may improve logistics, maximize participation, and reduce the idle time an operator may experience while waiting for his/ her turn to operate the machines.
  • Refer to this trainer's guide and explain your expectations of each operator.
  • Encourage participation, involvement, and questions.

minimum requirements

During the development of your training agenda, you will need to incorporate the following minimum requirements:

  • Operators must thoroughly read and understand the information in the telehandler Operator Training program which covers the topics required by ANSI/ITSDF B56.6, OSHA 1910.178 (I) and CSA B335.
  • Operators must demonstrate knowledge in the safe operation of a telehandler by completing the written exam at the end of the course. Exams require 100% accuracy.
  • Operators must demonstrate proficiency in the safe operation of a telehandler.

Operators must become familiar with the operator's manual(s) which will include opening and reviewing the organization of the operator's manual and discussing each section as it is covered in the class.

Another important minimum consideration is that operators need to be made aware of the following:

  • Both the operator's manual and the AEM Safety Manual provide a quick reference for operational, testing, and inspection activities that the operator must perform. These manuals should be considered a permanent part of the machine and should remain with the machine at all times. Machines must not be operated unless the proper manuals are in place.
  • Failure to obey the instructions and safety rules in these manuals and on machine decals could result in death or serious injury.
  • Operators are required by ANSI and CSA to read and understand their responsibilities before they operate a telehandler.

Operators must be informed that, in addition to the information found in the operator's manual and AEM Safety Manual, as well as machine decals and employers' instructions, they must use their own common sense as it relates to safety.

Additional requirements include the following:

  • Operators must be able to identify and avoid workplace hazards.
  • Operators must understand that the information provided in this course is not meant to cover all possible safety hazards and that they should ask their employer if they have any questions regarding safe work practices before proceeding.
  • The employer must keep a record that shows that each telehandler operator has been trained.
  • The record must include the name of the operator, date of training, date of evaluation, and the name of the person(s) giving the training and evaluation.
  • Training records must be kept for a minimum of three years.

training format

We suggest using several different techniques to capture the operator's attention and to create a learning environment that facilitates participation. These include:

  • Integrating written material with discussions of hands-on experiences. You are encouraged to add your own experiences to those of the operators.
  • For a learning experience to be meaningful, the dialogue and interaction must work both ways. Avoid a one-way monologue. Instead, draw the operators into the course content.

The Trainer's Guide will help you to present this program by offering an easy step-by-step instruction in sequential order. A large portion of the class is meant to be conducted verbally, but it is not intended to be merely read to the operators.

The Participant's Guide flows parallel to the Trainer's Guide, although a series of fill-in-the- blank statements are provided, which the operator is required to complete (answers are provided for the trainer in the Trainer's Guide).

These statements should reinforce key points of instruction as the course unfolds. You may provide the answers to some of the statements or call on the operators to fill in the blanks. This technique should encourage attentiveness and operator participation.

The video also repeats and reinforces key safety concepts and information.

Even though the course goes into considerable depth, it is not intended to cover every possible unsafe situation an operator may encounter. For this reason, it is critical to emphasize the importance of reading the appropriate operator's and safety manuals and decals on any telehandler prior to operation. This will serve to familiarize the participants with these materials and help emphasize their importance.

In addition, it should be made clear that it is the operator's responsibility to identify and avoid all hazards. If they are unclear about any possibly unsafe situation, they should stop work and seek the assistance of their employer.

It is your responsibility to provide each participant a copy of a telehandler operator's manual to use during the training and as a follow-up reference.

Sample copies of operator's manuals are included in the training kit. The trainer can use the samples to reproduce an operator's manual for each participant. Select a manual that is most appropriate for the type of telehandler used in the training.

Other operator's manuals are available free of charge by visiting www.genielift.com.

training location considerations

This training program consists of both classroom and hands-on activities. It is important that you identify, in advance, where each training segment will take place:

  • Use a well-lit and climate-controlled training room. Avoid hot conditions as this may detract from classroom attention.
  • Tables and desks should be arranged so that the operators do not feel cramped and so that there is easy access in and out of the classroom.
  • Consider having everyone sit in front of the class together. This will help create a more comfortable teaching environment.
  • The classroom should be large enough to provide everyone with adequate writing surfaces and space to lay out their materials.
  • The hands-on area should have ample space to set up the test track and for each operator to operate the machines safely.

Delivering Course Material

In this section we will provide a number of suggestions on how to improve your ability to communicate to your operators.

Of course, you will also need to determine how to improve your own abilities to communicate as you receive feedback from operators, colleagues, and fellow trainers.

Improve Communication to Operators

Effectively integrating the following into a training class will help make the class successful:

  • Be clear, consistent, and personal in the presentation of the materials.
  • Be clear and consistent with responses to questions. Don't be afraid to say "I don't know.“ It's better to answer honestly than to provide inaccurate information. If you are unable to answer a question, be sure to follow up with them with an answer, but always stick to the facts.
  • Make operators feel that what they have to say is important by being respectful and attentive.
  • Call operators by their first names and always use eye-to-eye contact when talking to them.
  • Involve everyone in the discussions. if someone is shy, ask them a question that they can't get wrong. This will boost their confidence and participation.
  • Lecturing to operators is not a good way to get their involvement into the course content. Avoid this method of presentation.

Stimulate Discussion

Create an environment that stimulates discussion. The following tips can help:

  • Let your operators know right away that it's okay to ask questions at any time during the training program.
  • Use the class's experiences in their work environment to support the safety points you are going to make.
  • Emphasize key points.
  • Encourage operators to engage in conversations with other operators on any topic of discussion during class breaks.
  • Open ended questions are a great way to stimulate two-way conversations.
  • Use the 5 Ws – (Who, What, Where, When, and Why).
  • Leave some time at the end of class for wrap-up questions.
  • Offer your personal experiences as another way to support your safety points.
  • Your operators are looking for feedback on their questions and experiences; interact with them.

Show Enthusiasm

Showing enthusiasm will energize the operators. Here are some ways you can do that:

  • Thank them for the time that they have dedicated to be there.
  • Tell them that you are excited to have them in the class with you today.
  • Change the volume and pace of your voice.
  • Move around the room.
  • Always face the operators when talking to them.

In short, try to put energy into the training environment.

Be an Effective Trainer

How does a trainer become effective? There isn't a simple answer to this question. However, successfully implementing the following tips will help:

  • Be well prepared. This is critical.
  • Have a good core knowledge of the content, machines, specifications, and agenda.
  • Anticipate lots of questions. Questions are good. Have the patience to answer each one accurately.
  • Sometimes materials covered earlier in the program may need to be covered again. Take the time to do this for your class to avoid confusion.
  • Accept the fact that not all classes will end on time. Don't create shortcuts to keep the class on schedule, just stay longer.

Be Confident

Being confident in the way you present your content will help you to gain the respect of the class participants.

To do this you should become very familiar with the telehandler(s), training content, applicable standards and supporting documentation, and media used.

Also, it is critically important to practice delivering the course materials so that you are comfortable with the flow and feel of the content.

Be Fair and Honest

Try to keep the class on schedule and flowing. Take breaks when needed but keep to the schedule as much as possible. An effective trainer should be fair and honest with the entire class, as well as:

  • Be clear and consistent
  • Treat everyone the same
  • Accept the fact that not everyone will be ready to pass the class. If, in your opinion, they do not meet the description of a qualified operator, then you should ask them to take the class again.

The Classroom Training Session

Provide each operator with a participant's guide, sample operator's manual, and pen or pencil.

Begin by playing the first section of the video. The video is included in both DVD and USB format. After each section, the video will instruct you to pause it and go back to the Trainer's/Participant's Guides. The Trainer's Guide will instruct you when to return to the next section of the video.

As you move through the program, participants will be prompted to fill in blank spaces with key concepts and information. You may want to ask for volunteers to offer suggestions before providing the correct response.

Make sure you take the time to answer all questions and verify that all participants understand the information.

When you come to the sections of the program that are reflected in the operator's manual, ask them to turn to that section in the operator's manual so they can become familiar with the detailed information provided.

Provide plenty of time for the written exam. Remember that each student works at their own pace. Try not to rush them. As students complete their exam, allow them to take a quick break while the others continue working.

The only passing score for the written exam is 100%. If any participant answers a question incorrectly, have them locate the correct answer in the Participant's Guide, correct the answer, and write the page number that contains the correct answer next to the question.

The hands-on training

Begin by physically demonstrating to the group the proper way to perform a workplace inspection, pre-operation inspection, and function test. Then demonstrate the practical operation of the forklift by actually performing the activities that they will be asked to repeat.

Have each participant perform the same activities in the same sequence while you evaluate their performance. Immediately capture your evaluation of their performance on the hands-on evaluation check sheet provided in this training kit.

Following the satisfactory completion of this course, each operator should receive a certificate of training and an operator's wallet card stating that they have successfully completed the telehandler Operator Training program.

Trainee Evaluation

A Trainee Evaluation Summary Log is provided in this Trainer's Kit. This log is used to record the performance information of each operator during the course and to provide proof of training to the employer.

Information captured includes:

  • written exam score
  • practical hands-on score
  • Pass/Fail results

The written exam score is simply the final score of the exam taken in the classroom.

The practical hands-on score is the total score taken from the hands-on evaluation check sheet and is based on the operator's demonstration of proficiency in key areas of safety, pre-operation and actual operation of the telehandler in a workplace environment.

All scores should be 100%. If an operator fails to receive 100% on a specific activity as outlined on the hands-on evaluation check sheet, review the activity with them and have them try again.

Remember, it's possible that not every operator will pass this course. It's your responsibility as a trainer to determine the proficiency of the operator as determined by their hands-on demonstration of safe machine operation.

Anything less than 100% is not acceptable.

certificate of training

On each certificate, the trainer should enter:

  • The name of the trainee
  • The name of the person or organization providing the training
  • The program sponsor (usually the employer)
  • The date of the training
  • The city and state ( or province) in which the training occurred

Operator's Card

On each operator's card, the trainer should enter the same information entered on the certificate of training as well as the model(s) of telehandler(s) the operator was trained to operate.

Please note:

This does not complete the training process. OSHA regulation 1910.178 (I)(6) states that:

"The employer shall certify that each operator has been trained and evaluated as required by this paragraph (I). The certification shall include the name of the operator, the date of the training, the date of the evaluation and the identity of the person(s) performing the training or evaluation."

To satisfy this requirement, an authorized officer of the operator's employer must also endorse the certificate of training.

The employer should retain a copy of the certificate(s) and training logs for their own records.

Policy and Guidelines

Everyone associated with the manufacturing, ownership, sale, rental and operation of rough terrain forklift equipment should be committed to safe and proper operation. Failure to observe established operating practices and to comply with required responsibilities could result in death or serious injury.

Equipment users and operators can make a major contribution towards safety if they:

1. Comply with employer, job site and government rules.

2. Read, understand and follow the instructions in this guide and other manuals supplied with the equipment or any attachments.

3. Use good safe work practices in a commonsense way.

4. Be alert and free from the influences of alcohol, drugs or medications that might affect eyesight, hearing, reactions or judgment.

5. Only have qualified operators who are trained and authorized, directed by informed and knowledgeable supervisors, running the machine.

Read and understand the operator’s manual and obey all safety rules and operating instructions before operating any rough terrain forklift equipment. The operator’s manual must be considered a permanent part of your equipment and must always remain with the product.

If there is any information in this guide that is not clear or which you believe requires modification, please contact us.

Appendix A — stability of Powered Industrial Trucks (Non-mandatory Appendix to Paragraph (l) of This Section)

A-1. Definitions.

The following definitions help to explain the principle of stability:

Center of gravity is the point on an object at which all of the object’s weight is concentrated. For symmetrical loads, the center of gravity is at the middle of the load.

Counterweight is the weight that is built into the truck’s basic structure and is used to offset the load’s weight and to maximize the vehicle’s resistance to tipping over.

Fulcrum is the truck’s axis of rotation when it tips over.

Grade is the slope of a surface, which is usually measured as the number of feet of rise or fall over a hundred foot horizontal distance (the slope is expressed as a percent).

Lateral stability is a truck’s resistance to overturning sideways.

Line of action is an imaginary vertical line through an object’s center of gravity.

Load center is the horizontal distance from the load’s edge (or the fork’s or other attachment’s vertical face) to the line of action through the load’s center of gravity.

Longitudinal stability is the truck’s resistance to overturning forward or rearward.

Moment is the product of the object’s weight times the distance from a fixed point (usually the fulcrum). In the case of a powered industrial truck, the distance is measured from the point at which the truck will tip over to the object’s line of action. The distance is always measured perpendicular to the line of action.

Track is the distance between the wheels on the same axle of the truck.

2-7

Wheelbase is the distance between the centerline of the vehicle’s front and rear wheels.

A-2. General.

A-2.1. Determining the stability of a powered industrial truck is simple once a few basic principles are understood. There are many factors that contribute to a vehicle’s stability: the vehicle’s wheelbase, track, and height; the load’s weight distribution; and the vehicle’s counterweight location (if the vehicle is so equipped).

A-2.2. The “stability triangle,” used in most stability discussions, demonstrates stability simply.

A-3. Basic Principles.

A-3.1. Whether an object is stable depends on the object’s moment at one end of a system being greater than, equal to, or smaller than the object’s moment at the system’s other end. This principle can be seen in the way a see-saw or teeter-totter works: that is, if the product of the load and distance from the fulcrum (moment) is equal to the moment at the device’s other end, the device is balanced and it will not move. However, if there is a greater moment at one end of the device, the device will try to move downward at the end with the greater moment.

A-3.2. The longitudinal stability of a counterbalanced powered industrial truck depends on the vehicle’s moment and the load’s moment. In other words, if the mathematic product of the load moment (the distance from the front wheels, the approximate point at which the vehicle would tip forward) to the load’s center of gravity times the load’s weight is less than the vehicle’s moment, the system is balanced and will not tip forward. However, if the load’s moment is greater than the vehicle’s moment, the greater load-moment will force the truck to tip forward.

A-4. The stability Triangle.

A-4.1. Almost all counterbalanced powered industrial trucks have a three-point suspension system, that is, the vehicle is supported at three points. This is true even if the vehicle has four wheels. The truck’s steer axle is attached to the truck by a pivot pin in the axle’s center. When the points are connected with imaginary lines, this three- point support forms a triangle called the stability triangle.

A-4.2. When the vehicle’s line of action, or load center, falls within the stability triangle, the vehicle is stable and will not tip over. However, when the vehicle’s line of action or the vehicle/ load combination falls outside the stability triangle, the vehicle is unstable and may tip over.

A-5. Longitudinal stability.

A-5.1. The axis of rotation when a truck tips forward is the front wheels’ points of contact with the pavement. When a powered industrial truck tips forward, the truck will rotate about this line. When a truck is stable, the vehicle- moment must exceed the load-moment. As long as the vehicle-moment is equal to or exceeds the load-moment, the vehicle will not tip over. On the other hand, if the load moment slightly exceeds the vehicle-moment, the truck will begin to tip forward, thereby causing the rear to lose contact with the floor or ground and resulting in loss of steering control. If the load-moment greatly exceeds the vehicle moment, the truck will tip forward.

A-5.2. To determine the maximum safe load- moment, the truck manufacturer normally rates the truck at a maximum load at a given distance from the front face of the forks. The specified distance from the front face of the forks to the line of action of the load is commonly called the load center. Because larger trucks normally handle loads that are physically larger, these vehicles have greater load centers. Trucks with a capacity of 30,000 pounds or less are normally rated at a given load weight at a 24-inch load center. Trucks with a capacity greater than 30,000 pounds are normally rated at a given load weight at a 36- or 48-inch load center. To safely operate the vehicle, the operator should always check the data plate to determine the maximum allowable weight at the rated load center.

A-5.3. Although the true load-moment distance is measured from the front wheels, this distance is greater than the distance from the front face of the forks. Calculating the maximum allowable load-moment using the load-center distance always provides a lower load- moment than the truck was designed to handle. When handling unusual loads, such as those that are larger than 48 inches long (the center of gravity is greater than 24 inches) or that have an offset center of gravity, etc., a maximum allowable load-moment should be calculated and used to determine whether a load can be safely handled. For example, if an operator is operating a 3000 pound capacity truck (with a 24- inch load center), the maximum allowable load-moment is 72,000 inch-pounds (3,000 times 24). If a load is 60 inches long (30-inch load center), then the maximum that this load can weigh is 2,400 pounds (72,000 divided by 30).

A-6. Lateral stability.

A-6.1. The vehicle’s lateral stability is determined by the line of action’s position (a vertical line that passes through the combined vehicle’s and load’s center of gravity) relative to the stability triangle. When the vehicle is not loaded, the truck’s center of gravity location is the only factor to be considered in determining the truck’s stability. As long as the line of action of the combined vehicle’s and load’s center of gravity falls within the stability triangle, the truck is stable and will not tip over. However, if the line of action falls outside the stability triangle, the truck is not stable and may tip over.

A-6.2. Factors that affect the vehicle’s lateral stability include the load’s placement on the truck, the height of the load above the surface on which the vehicle is operating, and the vehicle’s degree of lean.

A-7. Dynamic stability.

A-7.1. Up to this point, the stability of a powered industrial truck has been discussed without considering the dynamic forces that result when the vehicle and load are put into motion. The weight’s transfer and the resultant shift in the center of gravity due to the dynamic forces created when the machine is moving, braking, cornering, lifting, tilting, and lowering loads, etc., are important stability considerations.

A-7.2. When determining whether a load can be safely handled, the operator should exercise extra caution when handling loads that cause the vehicle to approach its maximum design characteristics. For example, if an operator must handle a maximum load, the load should be carried at the lowest position possible, the truck should be accelerated slowly and evenly, and the forks should be tilted forward cautiously. However, no precise rules can be formulated to cover all of these eventualities.

telehandler vs. Automobile

Operators must keep in mind the similarities and, more importantly, the differences between automobiles and telehandlers.

Similarities

Both, automobiles and telehandlers:

  • are equipped with four tires
  • are engine powered
  • have safety features
  • have visibility obstructions
  • utilize steering, brakes, horns, mirrors and

seat belts

Besides the similarities mentioned above, automobiles and telehandlers behave and operate very differently. The operator must be aware and alert at all times during machine operation.

4-3

Differences

Major differences which directly affect safety, machine operation and stability are:

Center of gravity: The center of gravity for telehandlers may change during operation, depending on the position of the machine, boom and the load. This can significantly affect the stability of the telehandler during operation. For automobiles, center of gravity is almost always constant.

Suspension: Telehandlers typically do not have any suspension systems. Automobiles have suspension systems designed to provide a comfortable ride.

Weight: Since telehandlers use large steel components as counterbalance, the weight of the telehandler is significantly greater than that of an average automobile.

Steering modes: Typically, only the front two wheels in automobiles assist in steering, whereas telehandlers have multiple steering modes (front wheel, four-wheel, crab steer and sometimes rear wheel).

stability

Unloading when raised

When the telehandler forks are loaded and the boom is raised from the stowed position, the center of gravity moves higher and towards the rear of the machine.

Unloading the telehandler when the boom is raised moves the center of gravity lower and toward the rear of the machine.

operation

Dynamic forces

telehandler operation, such as machine motion or moving suspended loads, creates dynamic forces on the machine and the load. Dynamic forces can suddenly shift the location of the combined center of gravity to outside of the stability triangle and cause the machine to become unstable.

Some conditions which may cause instability due to dynamic forces are:

Heavy braking: When a telehandler is traveling forward with a load and heavy braking is applied, the change in dynamic forces results in a sudden forward shift in the load's center of gravity. If this happens, the load may fall from the telehandler. If the load is fixed, the combined center of gravity may move outside the stability triangle, which could cause a forward tip-over.

High-speed turning: Turning the telehandler, with or without a load, causes lateral dynamic force which can result in a sideways shift in the center of gravity. If the telehandler turning speed is too high, the center of gravity may move outside the stability triangle, which could cause a side tip-over.

Driving on a slope cont.

Always travel perpendicular, or straight on, to the slope and always keep the machine in gear. Do not turn across a slope. Limit travel path and speed according to the condition of the ground surface, traction, slope, location of personnel and any other factors which may create a hazard.

Never drive the machine unless the telehandler and the load are in their proper travel position.

Procedure to follow during tip-over

During a tip over, the operator should remain in the cab and brace for impact by grasping the steering wheel and moving the body opposite to the direction of tipping.

All body parts should remain inside the operator’s cabin at all times.

  • Do not attempt to jump clear of the machine as this may result in serious injury or even death.
  • The seat belt must always be worn during machine operation. If the machine tips over while failing to wear a seat belt, serious injury or even death may occur.

Pedestrians

Always yield right-of-way to pedestrians. The telehandler operator must take care when working in areas with pedestrians or personnel, so as to not cause any bodily harm or injury. Use caution when approaching pedestrian areas. Before and during traveling, always look around for any pedestrians.

No person should be allowed to stand or pass under the elevated portion of the telehandler forks or attachment, whether the telehandler is unloaded or loaded.

Elevators

The operator must ensure that the elevator can support the telehandler and its load. Approach elevators slowly and ensure that the elevator is properly leveled with the building floor.

There should be adequate clearance for the telehandler to enter or leave the elevator.

Enter the elevator squarely with load end in front. Once the telehandler has entered the elevator, set the brakes and follow proper shut-down procedures.

Docks, trailers and railcars

In cases when a telehandler is operated on dock boards, bridge plates or rail cars, precautionary steps must be taken by the operator.

Dock boards:

  • Dock boards should be properly secured to prevent accidental movement during operation.
  • Dock boards should have a high friction surface for good grip.
  • All dock boards should have proper markings that mention capacity rating.
  • Dock boards should have substantial contact with the dock or loading platform and the transport vehicle or rail car to prevent rocking or sliding.
  • All surfaces that the telehandler will travel over must be capable of supporting the combined weight of the telehandler and the load.

Suspended Loads

Refer to the manufacturer’s specifications and Operator’s Manual on safe handling of suspended loads.

Whenever a telehandler is intended to be used to lift and transport a suspended load, the operator must be trained and qualified in identifying and avoiding the potential hazards in handling a suspended load on the site.

telehandler load charts are designed for loads where the load center is stationary. As a suspended load is moved, the load center can change. As a result, the standard load chart does not apply and extreme caution in transporting and lifting the load should be observed.

Only transport suspended loads with approved attachments by the telehandler manufacturer and do not transport suspended loads with chains or slings over the forks or carriage unless authorized by the telehandler manufacturer.

operation

The handling of suspended loads by means of a boom or other device can introduce dynamic forces affecting the stability of a rough terrain forklift truck that are not considered in the stability criteria of ANSI/ITSDF B56.6 Safety Standards.

Grades and sudden starts, stops, and turns can cause the load to swing and create a hazard if not externally stabilized.

If lifting and transporting a suspended load is permitted on the job site, the following criteria should be followed:

  • The user and the operator shall comply with all applicable jobsite, local, state, provincial, or federal rules, regulations, directives and standards related to the use of the truck with a suspended load.
  • The user shall ensure that the operating personnel are given the necessary training.

When handling suspended loads:

  • Speed must be limited by any conditions that could cause any unexpected movement of the load, or jeopardize the safe transport of the load
  • Do not exceed walking speed
  • During transport and lifting, use guide ropes or tag lines by qualified personnel to help control the load and prevent it from swinging
  • When visibility is or could be obstructed in the direction of travel, the operator must use alternative or additional means to safely transport the load, such as a spotter or a qualified signal person
  • Transport the load with the bottom of the load and the boom as low as possible
  • Only travel on solid surfaces

Raising and placing a load

The load chart in the cab shows the capacity limits of the telehandler. As mentioned previously, to use the load chart, the operator must know the weight of the load and how far out and up it is to be placed.

A telehandler may have more than one load chart. Be sure you are using the appropriate load chart that corresponds to the specific attachment and the telehandler model.

Procedure:

1. Travel to the desired final destination on the jobsite where you will be placing the load. Bring the telehandler to a gradual stop.

2. Place the transmission in neutral.

3. Apply the parking brake.

4. Lower the stabilizers, if equipped and if they are required for additional capacity.

5. Level the chassis, if equipped, with the leveling feature.

6. Gradually move the controller to raise the boom to the required height.

7. Gradually move the controller to land the load at the final position. Lower the load until the weight s completely off the forks. Do not apply a downward force with the forks.

8. Gradually move the controller to bring the forks out of the load.

9. When the forks are clear of the load and the structure, the boom can be retracted, then lowered.

Load retrieving with forks

1. Position the telehandler perpendicular with the load.

2. Spread the forks as wide as possible while still being able to pick up the load.

3. Level the forks to align with the entry point of the load.

4. Raise the forks to the proper load entry height.

5. Gradually move the controller to place the forks under the load.

6. Slightly raise the load so it is completely lifted free from its resting point.

7. Tilt the load back so that the fork carriage is supporting the load.

8. If picking from a ground position, carefully pick up the load, raise it to travel position and keep the forks tilted back slightly to cradle the load.

9. If lowering from an elevated position, completely lower the load by first retracting then lowering the boom to travel position.

Stacking and unstacking

Depending on workplace rules and regulations, an operator may be allowed to stack or unstack loads.

Beware of stack height limitations. Avoid over- stacking, as it may crush lower levels of loads or could cause the stack to become unstable.

While stacking, place loads only on a stable surface, which can sustain the weight of stacked loads. During unstacking, be aware of the clearance around the load and working area.

Refueling

Follow shut down procedures before refueling the telehandler. Never re-fuel if the engine is still running. Open flames and smoking are not permitted in proximity of refueling areas.

After refueling, tighten the fuel tank cap before starting the engine.

In case of spillage, ensure that the correct spill procedures are followed. Contact your local health and safety representative for more information.

Recharging Batteries

Follow shut down procedures before recharging the battery.

Open flames and smoking are not permitted in proximity of recharging areas.

Batteries contain acid. Avoid spilling or contacting battery acid. Neutralize battery acid spills with baking soda and water.

Ensure there is adequate ventilation for the dispersal of fumes from gassing batteries.

Shutdown

Telehandlers should be parked in designated areas. The parking location should be located away from potential hazards such as slopes, traffic flow exits, pedestrian walkways, elevators, ramps or fire-fighting equipment.

Follow shutdown procedures when the machine is left in an unattended state.

Note: A telehandler is considered unattended when –

  • The operator is 25 ft. or more away from the telehandler even if it is in view of the operator.
  • Whenever the telehandler is not in the view of the operator.

Procedure for parking the unit and shutting the machine down:

1. Park the telehandler in a safe location.

2. Bring the telehandler to a complete stop.

3. Apply the parking brake.

4. Fully retract the boom and lower the attachment. If equipped with forks, lower the forks flat on the ground to avoid a trip hazard.

5. Place all directional controls in neutral.

6. Turn off the telehandler.

7. Cycle the hydraulics to relieve any remaining pressure.

8. Remove the key.

9. If there is a separate fuel source, turn it off.

Shifting and Rotating

A shifting attachment can shift an entire load by shifting its fork carriage or forks to the right or left of the boom. The purpose of this attachment is to help improve the ability to precisely position the forks or carriage to pick or place a load.

A rotating carriage allows the operator to rotate the entire carriage, in many cases up to 10 degrees, clockwise or counterclockwise. The purpose of this attachment is to allow the operator to compensate for uneven load surface or uneven terrain when it affects an area that a load needs to be picked or placed.

Note: Never use the frame sway function to level an elevated load.

Swing Carriage

Swing carriages are typically used for helping land a load in tight areas, or maneuvering loads during travel around tight corners. Swing carriages allow the operator to move the carriage clockwise or counterclockwise along the horizontal axis.
