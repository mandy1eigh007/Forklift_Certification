SECTION 2: REGULATIONS 4 Classes 1-5 Training Overview
Classes 135 (Counterbalance Lift Trucks)
Warehouse and industrial forklifts operate with fixed vertical masts and fork carriages, handling loads on firm, level surfaces. Operators must be trained on safe handling practices, including proper load positioning and travel procedures.

Governing Standard: ANSI/ITSDF B56.6
In the USA, the American National Standards Institute and the Industrial Truck Standards Development Foundation (ANSI/ITSDF) have established safety requirements for the design, operation, and maintenance of powered industrial trucks. ANSI/ITSDF states: 'Only
trained and authorized persons shall be permitted to operate a rough terrain forklift truck. Operators shall be qualified as to their visual, auditory, physical, and mental ability to operate the equipment safely.'
Key training focus areas:




                                                                                                                                           When traveling with a load, forks should be positioned 436 inches above the ground with the mast tilted slightly backward
  Rear-wheel steering requires operators to be aware of tail swing clearance during turns




                                                                                                                                           Stability triangle principles for safe operation
  Load capacity verification using data plate specifications


        Why It Matters: Forklift tip-overs and struck-by incidents are among the leading causes of workplace fatalities. OSHA estimates that approximately 85 forklift fatalities and 34,900 serious injuries occur every year in the US. Proper training directly reduces
        these numbers.

SECTION 2: REGULATIONS 4 Class 7 Training Overview
Class 7 (Telehandlers)
       Learning Objective: By the end of this section, operators will understand the specialized skills required for telescoping boom equipment and apply safe practices unique to variable-reach telehandlers.

Telehandlers operate with telescoping booms and variable-reach capabilities, handling loads on rough terrain and construction sites. Operators must be trained on specialized handling practices unique to boom-equipped machines.

Key training focus areas:
       Boom extension effects on load capacity and stability


       Load chart interpretation for variable capacity calculations


       Outrigger deployment requirements for enhanced stability


       Rough terrain operation and ground condition assessment


       Construction site coordination and communication protocols


       Load moment indicator (LMI) system operation and warnings



       Why It Matters: Telehandlers have a dynamic, shifting center of gravity as the boom extends and rotates. Unlike a standard forklift, the safe lift zone changes with every boom position 4 making load chart literacy a life-safety skill, not just a compliance requirement.


       ¦   Key Safety Rule: Never extend the boom without first consulting the load chart 4 capacity changes dramatically with boom angle and extension.

How to Read a Telehandler Load Chart
A telehandler load chart shows maximum capacity at specific combinations of boom angle (degrees) and boom extension (feet). To use it correctly: (1) Identify your boom angle, (2) Find
your boom extension distance, (3) Read the intersection 4 that is your maximum allowable load weight. Never exceed this value. Always use the most conservative (lowest) capacity
value when conditions are uncertain.

       D   Remember: As boom extension increases, lifting capacity decreases 4 always check the load chart for the specific boom angle and extension combination before every lift.

SECTION 2: REGULATIONS 4 Safe Use Plan Overview
       Learning Objective: Operators and supervisors will understand the purpose and required components of a site-specific safe use plan, and recognize their responsibility to follow and update it.

Both forklift and telehandler operations require comprehensive site-specific safe use plans that address equipment capabilities, workplace hazards, and emergency procedures. These plans must be developed before operations begin and updated whenever conditions change.
Key plan components include:

  Equipment specifications and operating limitations                                                   Site-specific hazard identification and mitigation                                                      Traffic patterns and pedestrian safety protocols


  Emergency response procedures and communication                                                                                                           Operator training and certification requirements


ANSI/ITSDF B56.6-2021 4 Required Safe Use Plan Elements
1. Performing a site risk assessment to identify hazards, evaluate risk, develop control measures, and communicate the safe use program to affected persons
2. Verifying that the RTFL truck and attachment(s) being utilized are appropriate for the application
3. Planning for access, preparation, and maintenance of the site 4 including assessment that the support surface is adequate to support the weight of the RTFL truck
4. Verifying that RTFL truck inspections, maintenance, and repairs are performed per the provisions of this standard and as recommended by the manufacturer
5. Ensuring that only qualified and authorized personnel trained in accordance with OSHA 1910.178(l) are allowed to operate the RTFL truck
6. Ensuring the familiarization of trained, authorized RTFL truck operator(s) with the specific RTFL truck to be used
7. Informing the operator of local site requirements and warnings, and providing the means to protect against identified hazards in the areas where the RTFL truck will be operated
8. Monitoring the work being performed to ensure compliance with provisions of this standard
9. Preventing unauthorized use of the RTFL truck
10. Considering safety of persons in the vicinity of and not involved in the operation of the RTFL truck

       Why It Matters: A missing or outdated Safe Use Plan is not just a paperwork issue 4 it means operators may be working without knowing site hazards, ground conditions, or emergency procedures. ANSI/ITSDF B56.6-2021 requires this plan to exist in writing before any RTFL truck
       operation begins.


       D      Instructor Note: Emphasize that safe use plans are living documents 4 they must be reviewed and updated whenever equipment, site conditions, or personnel change.

SECTION 2: REGULATIONS AND OPERATOR TRAINING 4 OSHA 1910.178 (l)
OSHA standard 1910.178(l) establishes mandatory operator training requirements for all powered industrial truck operations in the United States. This regulation applies to both warehouse forklifts and rough-terrain telehandlers, though implementation details differ based on equipment type
and workplace conditions.

Certification (1910.178(l)(6))
The employer shall certify that each operator has been trained and evaluated. Certification must include: the operator's name, date of training, date of evaluation, and identity of the person(s) performing the training or evaluation.

Avoidance of Duplicate Training (1910.178(l)(5))
If an operator has previously received training in a topic and that training is appropriate to the truck and working conditions encountered, additional training in that topic is not required 4 provided the operator has been evaluated and found competent.

       Why It Matters: Incomplete training records are one of the most common OSHA citations in powered industrial truck operations. Employers who cannot produce written certification records for each operator face significant fines 4 and more
       importantly, operators without documented training may be working with dangerous knowledge gaps.

       '   Compliance Tip: Refresher training is required whenever an operator is observed operating unsafely, involved in an incident, or assigned to a different type of equipment.

SECTION 2: REGULATIONS AND OPERATOR TRAINING 4 CSA B335 Overview
Canadian Standard Association B335 provides comprehensive guidance for lift truck operator training in Canada, paralleling OSHA requirements while adding specific provisions for variable-reach equipment. This standard applies across all provinces and
territories, ensuring a consistent approach to lift truck safety and operation.
